# Texas Cultural Contexts — AVOIDED

Topics, scenarios, and language STAAR explicitly avoids — and our generator must avoid too.

**Provenance:** [CLAUDE-SYNTHESIZED] from observation of STAAR's neutrality stance, TEA assessment guidelines, and standard test-design ethics for K-12 high-stakes assessment. Verify any specific exclusion against TEA's published item-development guidelines if launching new content categories.

**Hard rule:** if a topic appears below, the generator must not produce questions with that content. The judge (CLAUDE.md §13/§25) will reject if it slips through; defense in depth.

---

## 1. Politics

### Never use:
- Names of currently-active or recently-active politicians (presidents in office during the kid's lifetime, governors, senators, congresspeople, party officials)
- Names of political parties (Republican, Democrat, Libertarian, Green, etc.)
- Political slogans, campaign messaging, or partisan phrases
- References to current legislation, current bills, current ballot measures
- Voting referenda, election outcomes within recent memory
- Immigration policy, abortion policy, gun policy as topics
- Confederate iconography or "Lost Cause" framing
- Any flag controversy, any monument controversy

### Historical figures who are OK in factual context:
- Founders (Washington, Jefferson, Madison, Hamilton) — in the context of the Constitution and early republic, not modern politics
- Lincoln, Grant, Lee, Davis — as historical actors of the Civil War era (their actions, not modern interpretive debates)
- 20th-century presidents in factual context (FDR's New Deal as economic event; LBJ's Texas roots; Eisenhower's WWII command)
- LBJ specifically — born in Texas, often referenced for that fact alone, NOT for Civil Rights / Vietnam / Great Society policy positions

**Test:** Could a parent of any political persuasion read this question and not feel their kid is being indoctrinated? If no → avoid.

## 2. Religion

### Never use:
- Religious figures (Jesus, Mohammed, Buddha, Moses, etc.)
- Religious holidays where the religious meaning matters (Christmas, Easter, Hanukkah, Ramadan, Diwali) — generic "winter holiday" / "spring break" / "celebration" is fine
- Religious texts (Bible, Quran, Torah)
- Faith-based morality framing (good/evil framed religiously)
- Specific places of worship as the focal context (church, mosque, temple, synagogue) — though incidental references are sometimes OK if not the topic
- Prayer, religious rituals, religious dietary restrictions
- Religious schooling, religious-affiliated organizations

### Acceptable in factual historical context only:
- Spanish missions in Texas history (architectural / historical fact, not religious-instruction)
- Religious factors in immigration history (the Pilgrims came for religious freedom; factual)
- Religious-named places (San Antonio, Corpus Christi) — they're place names, not religious topics

**Test:** Would a kid from a different faith (or no faith) feel singled out or excluded? If yes → avoid.

---

## 3. Contested history

### Never frame as the topic:
- Civil War "causes" debates beyond what TEKS 8.8A explicitly states (TEKS attributes the central cause to expansion of slavery; that's the official Texas position; do not introduce alternative framings in questions)
- Confederate "heritage" or "lost cause" narratives
- Texas Revolution as moral framing (the Alamo as "good vs evil"); factual events are OK
- Native American removal as "settlement" or "frontier development" — use neutral language, do not romanticize
- Slavery framed in any apologist way; factual mentions OK in TEKS 8.7A/8.8A context only
- Manifest Destiny as glorified or as evil; describe factually
- Reconstruction as "good" or "bad"; describe factually per TEKS 8.9A
- US-Mexican War causes / morality (factual events only)
- 20th-century race relations, civil rights movement leaders' modern interpretations

### Acceptable framing:
- Historical events as factual sequences
- Multiple perspectives presented neutrally
- "What does the source suggest?" questions where evidence is text-based
- Causes-and-effects as documented in TEKS

**Test:** Could this question be read by a Texan from any historical perspective without feeling their family's history is being maligned? If no → reframe.

## 4. Brand names and commercial content

### Never use:
- Specific real brand names (McDonald's, Walmart, Apple, Nike, Coca-Cola, Disney, etc.)
- Real product names (iPhone, Xbox, PlayStation, Roomba)
- Real company names in the role of "the example company" — use "a store", "a restaurant", "a bakery", generic
- Real video games, movies, TV shows, bands as topics
- Real social media platforms (Instagram, TikTok, YouTube)

### Acceptable:
- Generic commerce: "a store sells", "a bakery makes", "the family bought"
- Generic technology: "a tablet", "a computer", "a phone"
- Real local Texas establishments only when they are recognized landmarks (e.g., NASA Johnson Space Center is fine — it's a public institution, not a commercial brand)

## 5. Charged topics — never appropriate for K-12

- Violence: war atrocities, mass shootings, school shootings, terrorism, crime
- Drugs: any drug use, any drug-policy discussion, any addiction context
- Alcohol: drinking scenarios (even adults drinking responsibly is avoided)
- Tobacco: smoking, vaping
- Weapons (even sporting weapons in detail; "going hunting" is borderline; avoid)
- Death: family member death, pet death (rare older-grade exception; avoid for our generator), war casualties
- Divorce, family separation, custody issues
- Illness: serious illness in family members, terminal illness, mental illness as topic
- Poverty: kids in poverty as topic of question
- Homelessness
- Abuse: any form (physical, emotional, substance, sexual)
- Bullying as topic (briefly mentioned in passages OK; not a math/word-problem context)
- Body image, weight, body-shape, dieting
- Eating disorders
- Self-harm, suicide, depression
- Medical procedures in detail
- Sexual content of any kind (including kissing, dating-romance for younger grades; older-grade literary text may include light romantic context only)
- Pregnancy, childbirth (except abstract math contexts about generations)
- Reproductive biology beyond TEKS-required content

---

## 6. Specific real living people in unflattering contexts

- Don't name a real public figure as "the villain" or "the wrong-doer" of a scenario
- Don't use real local Texas figures (mayors, business owners, school principals) as characters
- Historical figures (Sam Houston, Stephen F. Austin) are OK in factual context per TEKS

---

## 7. Stereotype patterns to avoid

- Math abilities tied to ethnicity (Aanya can't be "naturally good at math because she's Asian"; Diego can't be "the Hispanic kid who likes spicy food")
- Gender-stereotyped activities (girls bake, boys build; AVOID this pattern)
- Income-tied stereotypes (Black/Hispanic characters don't always have less money or fewer resources in scenarios)
- Family-structure assumptions (don't always assume two-parent household; "Sofia and her grandmother" is fine; "Sofia and her mom and dad" fine; mix it up)
- Disability as the topic of a question (kids with disabilities are part of life; not a "lesson")
- Names matched to occupations stereotypically (Carlos doesn't always sell food trucks; Mateo doesn't always work with cars; mix it up)

## 8. Examples of borderline cases — how to handle

| Scenario | Allowed? | Why |
|---|---|---|
| "Maria walks to the mosque with her grandmother" | NO | Mosque is religious location as topic |
| "Maria walks to the library with her grandmother" | YES | Library is neutral civic institution |
| "Diego prays before dinner" | NO | Prayer as scenario element |
| "Diego eats dinner with his family" | YES | Family dinner is neutral |
| "After Easter, Diego counted his chocolate eggs" | NO (younger grades) | Easter is religious holiday |
| "After the spring festival, Diego counted his eggs" | YES | Generic celebration |
| "The Civil War was fought because of slavery" | YES (grade 8 + TEKS 8.8A explicitly) | Aligned with TEKS |
| "The Civil War was fought because of states' rights vs slavery" | NO | Introduces contested framing |
| "Sam Houston led Texas to independence" | YES | Factual TEKS 8.6A |
| "Sam Houston was a great Texan" | NO | Editorial / values framing |
| "An iPhone costs $X" | NO | Brand name |
| "A phone costs $X" | YES | Generic |
| "Sofia at the Alamo took a photo of the architecture" | YES | Architecture / tourism context |
| "Sofia at the Alamo learned about the brave Texans who died" | NO | Romanticized / contested framing |
| "Lucy goes hunting with her dad" | AVOID | Weapons context |
| "Lucy goes camping with her dad" | YES | Outdoor neutral |
| "The pastor of the church gave a speech" | NO | Religious figure |
| "The mayor of the town gave a speech" | YES | Civic figure |

## 9. Final guidance

When in doubt, ask: "Could this question be administered to every kid in Texas, regardless of family background, faith, political views, income, ethnicity, or family structure, without making any kid feel singled out, judged, indoctrinated, or excluded?"

If yes → ship it.
If no → reframe or skip.

The judge (`scripts/cold-start/judge.js`) will additionally screen for many of these via STATE_LEAK / AGE_FIT / and the standard kid-product safety filters. But the generator should produce STAAR-neutral content from the start, not rely on the judge to catch issues.
